2.1 ARPANET

Who

ARPANET (Advanced Research Projects Agency Network) was developed by the U.S. Department of Defense and is considered the precursor to the modern internet.

What

ARPANET was the first packet-switching network, allowing computers at different research institutions to communicate. This network laid the foundation for the internet.

Why

Its primary goal was to create a communication system that could survive disruptions and ensure resilience in case of an attack.

When

Established in 1969, the first successful message was sent between UCLA and Stanford. By 1983, ARPANET transitioned to using TCP/IP protocols, marking the official start of the modern internet.

How

ARPANET’s key innovations included packet-switching and the development of TCP/IP protocols. Read more on the Internet Society’s History Page.

2.2 Vint Cerf & Bob Kahn

Who

Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn, known as the "fathers of the internet," co-authored TCP/IP, which became the foundation of internet communication.

What

Their development of TCP/IP allowed different networks to communicate seamlessly, forming the backbone of the internet.

Why

TCP/IP protocols were essential for enabling reliable data transmission across independent networks.

When

They published their paper on TCP/IP in 1974. By 1983, TCP/IP became the standard for ARPANET, establishing the modern internet.

How

Cerf and Kahn’s work on open-architecture networking allowed networks to interconnect globally. Learn more about Vint Cerf on the Google Research Page.

Example:

For their contributions, Cerf and Kahn received the Turing Award in 2004. Discover their story here.

2.3 Early Programs Used on ARPANET

Who

Researchers developed early programs like email, FTP, and Gopher to explore the potential of networked communication.

What

Email and FTP were among the first applications, allowing for message exchanges and file transfers. Gopher was an early attempt at creating a text-based internet navigation system.

Why

These programs facilitated collaboration among institutions connected to ARPANET and paved the way for the World Wide Web.

When

Email was invented in 1971, while FTP became a standard for file transfers by 1973. Gopher was introduced in 1991 but was soon replaced by the World Wide Web.

How

Programs used packet-switching to transmit data across nodes, allowing for efficient communication. Learn more about early applications here.
